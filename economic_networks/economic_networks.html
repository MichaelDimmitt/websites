Understanding your city

Know your economic landscape

Every city is a living network of institutions, capital, talent, and influence. Understanding how it works is the first step toward contributing to it.

How could you contribute to your economy? You might not directly participate in every node of this network — you probably aren't running a port, sitting on a zoning board, or managing a hospital system. But you live inside this web of relationships whether you see it or not.

The money that pays for your roads came from somewhere. The job you work exists because of decisions made by people in these networks. The restaurant you love survives because of foot traffic created by development patterns, tourism flows, and institutional anchors.

Go visit these places. Walk through your downtown. Drive past the port. Sit in on a city council meeting. Tour the college campus. Eat at the businesses on the main corridor. See who's building what, and where the cranes are. Watch where the people flow.

You don't need to be a mover and shaker to matter. But you should develop respect for the economic network of where you live — because once you see how the pieces connect, you start to notice where you fit, where you could help, and where the gaps are.
Network 01
Jacksonville, Florida

Jacksonville economic & socioeconomic network

Jacksonville's economy is anchored by three pillars: the military (Naval Station Mayport), healthcare (Mayo Clinic), and financial services (FIS, Black Knight). These base industries bring outside dollars in, which then circulate through local businesses, real estate, and services.

Notice the network brokers at the center — the connectors who sit on multiple boards, bridge disconnected groups, and quietly shape which deals get done. In a city of nearly a million people, these structural bridges are what keep the network from fragmenting into silos.

Network 02
St. Augustine, Florida

St. Augustine economic & socioeconomic network

St. Augustine tells a fundamentally different story. Here, tourism is the gravitational center — 7 million visitors a year pour money into hotels, tours, dining, and attractions. The bed tax loop (visitors → hotels → TDC marketing → more visitors) is the engine that keeps everything spinning.

The tension between preservation and development defines this city. HARB — the Historic Architectural Review Board — acts as a gate on every building project in the historic district. In a city of just 15,000 residents, the handful of people who sit on overlapping boards wield enormous quiet influence.

Every city has a network like this. Learn yours.
