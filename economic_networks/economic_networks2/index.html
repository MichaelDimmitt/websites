Jacksonville economic & socioeconomic network

What to notice

Military-civic axis: Naval Station Mayport channels federal dollars through the mayor's office into local services — a flow that doesn't depend on the private economy at all.

Fintech cluster: FIS and Black Knight (now ICE) are both headquartered here, creating a self-reinforcing loop: fintech jobs attract talent, talent demands housing, developers build, banks finance — a virtuous cycle that the Chamber of Commerce actively lubricates.

JAXPORT as base industry: The port is Jacksonville's purest export-economy node. It pulls in trade finance from banks and infrastructure dollars from the mayor, then pushes supply chain jobs into local services. Every container that moves through JAXPORT is new money entering the city.

St. Augustine economic & socioeconomic network

What to notice

The tourism flywheel: Visitors generate room revenue for hoteliers, who pay bed tax (5%) to the TDC, which spends it on marketing to attract more visitors. It's a closed-loop amplifier — and the single most important cycle in the entire economy.

Preservation as gatekeeper: HARB and the Historical Society don't just preserve buildings — they regulate what the entire commercial core looks like. Every developer must pass through the design review gate. This constrains supply, raises prices, and keeps the character that tourism depends on. It's simultaneously the city's greatest asset and its biggest growth bottleneck.

Flagler College as cultural engine: Unlike a research university, Flagler's contribution is cultural — graduates feed the arts and creative scene, student spending sustains St. George Street, and the college itself is a tourist attraction (the former Hotel Ponce de León).

Madrid economic & socioeconomic network

What to notice

The museum triangle as anchor: The Prado, Reina Sofía, and Thyssen-Bornemisza aren't just cultural assets — they function as a tourism anchor that draws 10M+ visitors per year. Unlike Jacksonville's military base or St. Augustine's historic district, Madrid's anchor is a curated cultural brand that competes globally with Paris and London.

Three-tier government funding: Madrid has a funding cascade that neither Florida city has — EU recovery and cohesion funds flow into the Comunidad de Madrid, which funds universities and hospitals, which produce the talent that feeds the private economy. This external injection is a structural advantage.

Barajas as logistics multiplier: Adolfo Suárez Madrid–Barajas is Spain's primary international airport and a major European hub. It functions like JAXPORT but for air freight and business travel — creating demand for financial and consulting services, feeding supply chains to mercados and retail, and connecting the city to global capital flows.

Barrio associations as community layer: Spain's asociaciones de vecinos (neighborhood associations) play a much stronger civic role than American equivalents. They lobby the Ayuntamiento directly and help sustain the mercado system that anchors neighborhood identity — a bottom-up force that counterbalances developer-driven change.

The pattern across all three

Despite enormous differences in scale, language, and history, all three cities run on the same anatomy: anchor institutions that can't leave, base industries that bring in outside money, capital allocators that decide what gets built, civic leaders that set the rules, local businesses that recirculate spending, creative talent that attracts investment, network brokers that connect everyone, and community organizations that give residents a voice.

What differs is the proportions. Jacksonville is logistics-and-fintech weighted. St. Augustine is tourism-dominated. Madrid is a diversified capital city where government and finance are co-dominant. These proportions determine each city's resilience, velocity, and trajectory — the outcome layer of the urban economy taxonomy.

Scale changes everything — and nothing

Madrid is 450× the population of St. Augustine, yet both depend on tourism as a major base industry. The difference is that Madrid's tourism coexists alongside finance, government, and logistics — so a tourism downturn hurts but doesn't collapse the system. St. Augustine has no such cushion. Jacksonville sits in between: diversified enough to survive the loss of any single sector, but not so diversified that it could absorb a simultaneous hit to military spending and fintech.

The network structure, though, is remarkably consistent. Every city has brokers spanning structural holes. Every city has anchor institutions whose fate is tied to the place. Every city has a civic layer that mediates between capital and community. The taxonomy holds.
